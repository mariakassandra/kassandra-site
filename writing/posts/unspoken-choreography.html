The Unspoken Choreography of Strangers Avoiding Eye Contact on Public Transportation

2025

There's a performance happening daily on public transit that makes Marcel Marceau look like an amateur—the intricate dance of pretending no one else exists while pressed against strangers' bodies in ways that would otherwise constitute a relationship milestone.

I've cataloged the techniques with the dedication of someone who clearly needs better hobbies:

The Window Gaze
Staring at your reflection with such intensity you'd think your face contains the secrets of the universe, not just evidence of that hastily applied morning concealer. The true artists maintain this fascination even when the window view changes from "dim reflection of myself" to "actual scenery," proving humans will literally prefer watching themselves look miserable over making eye contact with Brad from accounting.

The Phone Scroll
An Olympic sport where medals are awarded for how long you can pretend to read emails you've already read. I once watched a woman scroll through her entire camera roll twice, lingering on blurry photos of her ceiling fan rather than acknowledge the man whose briefcase was essentially proposing to her kneecap.

The Middle Distance Meditation
Perfecting that thousand-yard stare that suggests either profound philosophical depth or early-onset dissociation. My personal favorite, as it allows me to appear thoughtful while actually ranking breakfast cereals by potential effectiveness as boat-building materials.

The Bag Inspection
Suddenly becoming fascinated by the contents of your bag, examining your keys like they're artifacts in the Museum of Personal Belongings. "Is this the same chapstick I had yesterday? Better check it from seven different angles."

The choreography reaches its crescendo when someone needs to exit. Bodies that have been pretending not to share oxygen must coordinate a complex series of movements while maintaining the fiction they exist in separate dimensions. It's like watching a contact improv class taught by someone whose only instruction was "don't admit these other people are humans."

What fascinates me most is how universal these behaviors are. I've watched this same performance in New York, Manila, and Atlanta—different stages, identical choreography. There's something almost beautiful about this collective agreement to respect each other's solitude while being compressed together like existential sardines.

The true irony? We've all mastered looking everywhere except at each other, creating the most intimate daily experience shared by people determined not to share an experience.
